Spoovel Mechanism · Fox’s Anvil Blueprint

Spoovel / Spuval / Spooval / Spovel — a low-tech, field-repairable, sack-filling and binding tool for restoration, flood response, and community resilience.

Section 2

Functional Brief

2.1 Purpose

The Spoovel Mechanism is a low-tech, modular tool intended to make the repeated task of filling, closing, and deploying sacks:

  • Faster and more consistent than fully manual methods.
  • Less physically punishing for workers and volunteers.
  • Less dependent on diesel-heavy equipment and fragile supply chains.

Typical use-cases include:

  • Flood response and temporary levee construction.
  • Erosion control and slope stabilization.
  • Soil and compost movement for restoration projects.
  • Seed, biochar, or amendment mixes for agroecology.

2.2 Scale & Modularity

The core Spoovel unit is designed for:

  • Two–four human operators working in rotation.
  • Portable deployment in the back of a truck, on a trailer, or on-site frames.
  • Construction from common steel profiles, bicycle parts, and scrap components.

Additional modules (such as a Commons Conveyor, sorting screens, or a MedicineWheel data interface) can be bolted on without redesigning the core mechanism.

2.3 Key Design Principles

  • Low-tech / high-resilience: Prefer gravity, levers, cams, and pedals over electronics.
  • Field-repairable: All wear points accessible; replacement parts sourceable locally.
  • Repeatable cycle: A clear, repeatable motion that workers can learn quickly.
  • Ergonomic: Reduce awkward bending, twisting, and lifting where possible.
Section 3

Mechanism Overview

3.1 Major Subsystems

The Spoovel Mechanism can be understood as four primary subsystems:

  • Frame & Sack Station: Fixed structure holding sacks at working height.
  • Material Intake & Drop: Where soil or material is guided into the sack mouth.
  • Cycling Arm / Cam System: Converts human input (pedal, handle, or crank) into a regular cycle.
  • Closure & Release: How the sack is twisted, tied, or otherwise sealed and released.

3.2 Frame & Sack Station

  • Built from steel angle, tube, or repurposed rack material.
  • Front station features hooks or clamps to hold sack tops open.
  • Height chosen so operators can stand upright with minimal forward bend.
  • Optional footplate or platform for worker stability and leveling on rough ground.

3.3 Material Intake

  • A funnel or chute guides material from shovels, small loaders, or conveyors.
  • Chute mouth is wide enough for two shovelers to work without clashing.
  • Chute exit is positioned just above the open sack mouth.
  • Optional “spoiler” plates inside the chute diffuse impact and reduce sack tearing.

3.4 Cycling Arm / Cam

A simple cam wheel, crank, or rocking beam governs the rhythm of the machine. Human input can be:

  • Foot pedal (treadle-style).
  • Hand lever or crank.
  • Tandem arrangement where one operator pedals while others shovel and handle sacks.

The cam does not move the soil; it moves the work around the sack: clamping, shaking, twisting, and releasing.

3.5 Closure & Release

  • A pair of opposed arms or grippers hold the top of the sack and twist or fold it as the cam cycles.
  • A simple tie or clip mechanism (wire, cord, or strap) is engaged at the end of the twist.
  • The cam then opens the grippers and allows the sealed sack to drop to a waiting pallet or slide.
Section 4

Functional Cycle (One Example)

This section describes a possible full cycle for one Spoovel unit. Actual implementations may vary. 

1. PREPARE
   - Empty sack is placed on hooks/clamps at the front station.
   - Operator checks that chute is clear and pallet/slide is in position.

2. FILL
   - One or two workers shovel material into the intake chute.
   - Material drops through the chute into the sack.
   - A gentle shake, governed by the cam or a foot tap, helps settle material.

3. LEVEL / CHECK
   - Operator visually checks approximate fill level (by line, weight feel, or simple gauge).
   - If under-filled, additional shovels are added; if overfilled, excess is removed.

4. CLOSE
   - Operator gathers the top of the sack and places it into the closure arms.
   - With a single pedal/lever stroke, the cam engages:
       - Arms twist, fold, or pinch the sack top into a consistent closure shape.

5. TIE / SECURE
   - Tie material (wire, cord, strap) is looped and cinched around the twisted/closed section.
   - This can be manual or assisted by a simple tie guide on the frame.

6. RELEASE
   - Cam completes its cycle and opens the arms.
   - Sack drops to pallet/slide.
   - Operator clears station and hangs the next empty sack.

7. RESET
   - Pedal/lever returns to neutral.
   - Frame and chute are visually checked before repeating the cycle.
Section 5

Materials & Build Notes (High-Level)

The Spoovel Mechanism is intentionally specified at a high level so that local builders can adapt to available materials.

5.1 Typical Material Sources

  • Scrap steel angle, tube, or channel for the frame.
  • Old bicycle frames, cranks, or hubs for cam and pedal assemblies.
  • Reclaimed conveyor or chute pieces for the material intake.
  • Springs, hinges, or clamps from agricultural or workshop scrap bins.

5.2 Tools Required (Minimum)

  • Welder (MIG or stick) or strong bolted construction methods.
  • Angle grinder or hacksaw for cutting steel.
  • Drill and bits for pivot points and bolt holes.
  • Measuring tools, square, and basic layout tools.

5.3 Safety Considerations

  • All moving parts should have guards where pinch or crush hazards exist.
  • Operators should be trained in the cycle and shown safe zones around the frame.
  • Emergency stop: a simple way to disengage or block the cam in case of trouble.
  • Regular inspection of welds, pivots, and hooks for fatigue and cracking.
Section 6

Adaptation & Field Notes

Builders and field crews are encouraged to treat this schematic as a starting point, not a rigid blueprint.

  • Record what works well and what fails under real conditions.
  • Photograph and sketch modifications; share them back to the Commons.
  • Adapt the frame height and stance for local body sizes and terrain.
  • Explore modular mounts: truck bed, trailer, ground frame, barge, etc.

A MedicineWheel-style knowledge sharing system may be used to gather versions of the Spoovel from many regions, climates, and communities. The goal is not a single “perfect” design but a network of related, interoperable tools tuned to local realities.
